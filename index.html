Mark Twain

(1835-1910)

Public domain image from Wikimedia Commons

Mark Twain's wit, empathy, and gift for satire have made an indelible mark on American literature (and even, for those who believe in such a thing, on the American character). Although he achieved enormous success in his lifetime, Twain never lost his unpretentious nature, and his finely-tuned ability to detect the B.S. of others has bequeathed to the rest of us a hilarious and instructive collection of epigrams. Here are just a few:

  • "'Classic.' A book which people praise and don't read."
  • "Often it does seem a pity that Noah and his party did not miss the boat."
  • "The holy passion of Friendship is of so sweet and steady and loyal and enduring a nature that it will last through a whole lifetime, if not asked to lend money."
  • “I once sent a dozen of my friends a telegram saying 'flee at once – all is discovered.' They all left town immediately.”
  • "The political and commercial morals of the United States are not merely food for laughter, they are an entire banquet."

You can read more about Mark Twain's life on Wikipedia, explore his written works on The Literature Network and Project Gutenberg, or find more quotations on Twainquotes.com.
